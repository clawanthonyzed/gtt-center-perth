# GTT Center Perth — Overview for Imara

**Prepared:** 2026-07-20 | **Purpose:** A plain-language overview of the GTT Center Perth concept, why it's expected to be profitable, and where it currently stands — for Anthony to walk through with Imara.

> A note on how to read this: every number below is a planning estimate based on research and modelling, not real trading results — there is no venue open yet. Where a figure is uncertain or still needs confirming from a third party (like the pathology lab), that's said plainly rather than glossed over. Full technical detail and sourcing for every figure here lives in `docs/executive-summary.md` and `docs/business-plan.md`, which this document is a friendlier front door to.

## The Idea, in One Paragraph

Every pregnant woman in Australia does a mandatory glucose tolerance test around 24-28 weeks — a 2 to 2.5 hour appointment involving three blood draws with waiting in between, usually spent sitting in an ordinary pathology waiting room. GTT Center Perth turns that unavoidable wait into a wellness experience: while the blood draws happen on schedule, the woman gets a massage, a manicure, her hair done, or a facial — whichever combination she picks — instead of just sitting there. As far as this venture's research has found, nothing like it currently exists in Western Australia. One comparable business exists in Melbourne, and it's reportedly booked out weeks in advance.

---

## Why It's Expected to Be Profitable

**The short version:** the test itself already guarantees a steady stream of women who have to come through the door — this isn't a business that has to create demand from nothing, it's a business built around demand that already exists every single day, for medical reasons, regardless of the economy or marketing spend. The only question is whether enough of those women choose GTT Center Perth over an ordinary pathology clinic, and whether the wellness side of the business can be run profitably alongside the clinical side.

**The numbers, once the venue is up and running at a steady pace (a realistic ramp-up period is expected first):**

| | Amount |
|---|---|
| Monthly profit, once running at a steady, established pace | **approximately +A$25,000/month** |
| Annualised, at that same steady pace | **approximately +A$301,000/year** |
| Monthly costs that need to be covered before any profit is made | **approximately A$88,600/month** (staff wages, rent, and other running costs) |

**Break-even, done properly — by revenue, and by the two different types of client, not one blended headcount:**

A single "how many visits per month" number hides an important detail: GTT Center Perth actually serves two quite different kinds of client, who spend very different amounts, and treating them as one interchangeable unit gives a misleading picture. The honest way to look at it:

| Client type | Typical spend per visit | What "covering costs" looks like *if that were the only kind of client* (illustrative bookend, not the real plan) |
|---|---|---|
| Morning GTT clients (the clinical test + wellness package) | ~A$250 per visit (conservative pricing) | Would need ~355 visits/month on its own — far more than the venue can physically fit in the morning slots available (the model caps morning capacity at 10/day, ~220/month) |
| Afternoon wellness-only clients (no clinical test, individual services) | ~A$95 per visit | Would need ~933 visits/month on its own — well beyond what a small afternoon team could realistically deliver |

**Neither of these extremes is the actual plan — the real model blends both:** roughly 220 morning GTT visits/month plus roughly 350 afternoon wellness-only visits/month, together bringing in the ~A$88,600/month needed to cover costs, with room to spare (that's where the ~A$25,000/month profit comes from). The point of showing the two bookends above isn't that either is realistic on its own — it's to make clear that a GTT visit and an afternoon wellness visit are not equivalent, and any future volume target should say which kind of client it means, not just "visits." Full detail, including the actual blended monthly figures month-by-month, is in `docs/profit-loss-tables.md`.

These figures already assume a conservative package price for the morning packages (the lower of the two price tiers on offer), not an optimistic best case. If the mix of bookings skews toward the higher-priced package, or if prices are increased once the venue has a track record, the real result could be better than this.

**Where the money comes from:** the clinical blood-test part of the business is handled by an external pathology partner and earns GTT Center Perth nothing directly — that part exists purely to bring women through the door. 100% of GTT Center Perth's own revenue comes from the wellness services (massage, nails, hair, facials) sold as fixed-price packages in the morning or as individual services in the afternoon, plus a smaller amount from cafe/retail add-ons.

**How the morning and afternoon connect — this isn't wasted staff time:** the treatment staff (massage therapists, nail technicians, hairdressers, beauty therapists) rostered for the morning GTT shift aren't fully occupied the whole time — each client's own service only takes part of the 2-2.5 hour visit. The model is built so that same morning team can also take **standalone, non-GTT bookings during the gaps between their GTT clients' services** — someone who just wants a manicure, not the blood test, can be slotted into that same morning downtime rather than needing an entirely separate afternoon-only booking. This is on top of, not instead of, the dedicated afternoon-only wellness shift described above.

**What could make this better or worse than modelled — an honest range, not just a rosy number:**
- **Better case:** if bookings lean toward the pricier service package, or the afternoon-only wellness slots (which don't need the clinical test at all) fill up more than currently modelled, monthly profit could be meaningfully higher.
- **Worse case:** if demand comes in well below the modelled level — say, only 70% of the target volume with none of the afternoon business — the venue would run at a real loss in that scenario, not just break even. This is why the marketing/waitlist-building work (getting the word out to midwives and doctors who refer patients) matters as much as the physical setup.
- The full best/base/worst breakdown, not just the headline number, is in `docs/break-even-sensitivity-analysis.md`.

## What Has to Happen Before It Can Open

In plain order of what's actually blocking what:

1. **Find and secure a physical venue.** This is the single thing everything else is waiting on right now. No staff can be hired, no fit-out can begin, until a location is locked in.
2. **Confirm the pathology partnership.** The lab that will do the actual blood draws has been approached (an email has gone out to the preferred partner) and a reply is being awaited. Two backup options exist if the first choice doesn't work out.
3. **Hire a Venue Manager.** This is the first staff hire once a venue is secured — the on-site person who runs day-to-day operations, handles any medical emergency response, and manages the rest of the team. The job posting is already written and ready to go.
4. **Fit out the space, hire the rest of the team, and do a soft trial run** before opening to the public.

None of this has a fixed calendar date attached on purpose — the plan is to move through these steps in the right order rather than force a deadline that doesn't match reality.

## What's Genuinely Still Uncertain (Not Hidden, Just Honest)

- The exact cutoff time the pathology lab needs specimens by each day — this affects how many clients can realistically be seen each morning, and is still awaiting the lab's reply.
- The precise startup capital figure, pending a real venue and real fit-out quotes.
- Whether prices should increase after a few months of trading, and if so, when — there's a documented comparison of the options (`docs/price-increase-comparison.md`), but no decision has been made yet since there's no trading data to base it on.

None of these are the kind of "we don't actually know if this works" uncertainty — they're normal, expected unknowns for a pre-launch venture that will resolve themselves as the venue gets closer to opening.
