## What Worked
- **Booking-driven rostering** — since clients select services at booking time, staff can be rostered to confirmed bookings rather than a blanket calendar assumption. This unlocked the whole AM capacity improvement.
- **Formal verified timetables** (Scenario B → C → D) — building the actual per-client draw/service schedule and checking every staff member for double-booking, rather than estimating from aggregate numbers. This caught real problems before they became commitments.
- **Checking package pricing against real market comparables** (Perth day spa pricing) rather than assuming — confirmed GTT's A$250/A$300 packages are not underpriced.
- **Direct regulatory research** (Fair Work award text, WA Retail Trading Hours Act) instead of assuming penalty rates or trading restrictions — corrected a wrong assumption about Sunday trading law and confirmed the Saturday penalty rate.

## What Didn't Work — Don't Repeat These
1. **First PM staffing estimate assumed casual staff work a full blanket shift regardless of actual bookings.** This overstated PM labor cost by roughly 2x and made the whole venture look loss-making. Fix: cost casual/bolt-on labor on **actual hours worked** (sessions ÷ realistic throughput), not shift-length presence. This flipped the venture from a projected loss to profitable at Month 4.
2. **First Saturday AM costing used the same "monthly salary ÷ 22" method as weekday staff.** Saturday is an *additional* bolt-on shift beyond the normal weekly contract — it needed the same hours-based method as PM, not a slice of ongoing salary. Got this wrong initially, showed a false Saturday loss (-A$790/day), corrected to a real profit (+A$1,580.50/day conservative).
3. **First attempt at the 3-chair timetable (Scenario D) used a shared tight stagger across all 3 chairs simultaneously**, which created a real, unresolvable double-booking collision. Fix: let each chair run its own already-proven independent 40-minute rhythm (different phlebotomists can draw at the same literal minute — that was never actually a constraint). This is what Anthony's "simultaneous starts are fine as long as capacity/services are available" clarification unlocked.
4. **Asserting Fair Work award rates from memory without checking** — an earlier "Saturday 1.33x" figure was stated before being sourced; always verify against the actual current award text or Fair Work's calculator before building financial models on it.

**General pattern to watch for:** before costing any new staffing scenario, first classify it — is this an ongoing weekday/contracted role (cost via monthly salary ÷22) or a bolt-on additional shift (cost via actual hours worked)? Getting this wrong understated profitability twice in this session alone.

## Regulatory Findings (Confirmed, Sourced)
- WA Retail Trading Hours Act: hairdresser/beautician/barber shops are explicitly exempt (Section 5) — no legal restriction on start times any day, including Sunday. Sunday closure here is Anthony's business choice, not a legal requirement — the reopening trigger is proven, profitable standalone PM demand, not a permanent decision.
- Hair and Beauty Industry Award MA000005: Saturday penalty **confirmed** — 133% (permanent) / 150% (casual). Sunday 200%. Public holiday 250–275%.
- Health Professionals Award MA000027 (phlebotomists): a clause exists suggesting Saturday 8am–4:30pm may count as *ordinary hours* for private pathology practices — **unconfirmed**, needs a payroll advisor or Fair Work direct check. All financial models use the conservative (full penalty) assumption until this is verified.
