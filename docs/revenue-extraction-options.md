## Structural Starting Point

GTT Center Perth trades under **YETI Holding Trust** (a discretionary trust), with **YETI Tipi Holdings PTY LTD** as corporate trustee. Revenue from the venture flows into the trust, not to Anthony or Imara directly, and not automatically to either of them as income. Getting money out of a discretionary trust to an individual requires one of the mechanisms below — money doesn't move from "trust bank account" to "Anthony's or Imara's pocket" without one of these steps happening.

**Anthony and Imara's specific relationship to the trust, as already established in this venture's documents (`financial-model.md`, `research.md`):**
- Anthony — Trustee (non-operational; not doing hands-on GTT Center Perth work)
- Imara — funding partner (provided the ~A$200K self-funding capital); also not operationally involved day-to-day (the Venue Manager, a separate hire, runs the venue)

Neither is currently described as an employee of GTT Center Perth. This matters because it changes which mechanisms are actually available to each of them (see below).

## Mechanism 1 — Trust Distribution (Beneficiary Distribution)

**What it is:** at the end of each financial year (before 30 June), the trustee resolves to distribute the trust's income to one or more beneficiaries. Each beneficiary then declares that distributed amount as their own assessable income and pays tax on it at their individual marginal rate.

**General mechanics:**
- Requires a formal trustee resolution before 30 June each year — this is a hard, missable deadline (a late resolution can mean the trust itself pays tax on undistributed income at the top marginal rate, which is punitive).
- Each beneficiary named in the trust deed can potentially receive a distribution (subject to the deed's terms) — this is the mechanism that allows "income splitting" across beneficiaries, each using their own $18,200 tax-free threshold and lower marginal-rate brackets.
- No wage/PAYG withholding involved — it's a share of trust income, not a salary.
- Requires the beneficiary to actually be entitled to receive that income under the trust deed — a solicitor/accountant needs to confirm Imara's specific status as a beneficiary (funding partner is not automatically the same thing as a named beneficiary — this needs direct confirmation against YETI Holding Trust's actual deed, not assumed).

**General tax treatment:** each beneficiary's distributed share is included in their own individual tax return and taxed at their marginal rate (0% up to $18,200, then the standard individual brackets). Losses do not distribute out — they stay trapped in the trust.

**Where this is relevant to Anthony and Imara specifically:** this is the mechanism most consistent with how this venture's revenue model was designed from the outset (revenue flows to the trust, "never to Anthony personally" per standing rule) — it's the natural fit for a trustee/beneficiary structure where neither is doing hands-on operational work.

---

## Mechanism 2 — Wages/Salary (If Operationally Active)

**What it is:** if either Anthony or Imara actually performs work for GTT Center Perth (management, oversight duties, etc. — beyond passive trustee/funding roles), the trust (as employer) can pay them a wage or salary for that work, the same way it pays any other employee.

**General mechanics:**
- Requires PAYG withholding, superannuation guarantee contributions (12% from 1 July 2025), and normal employer obligations (payslips, STP reporting) — same compliance burden as any other GTT Center Perth employee.
- The wage must reflect genuine work actually performed, at a commercially reasonable rate for that work — the ATO scrutinises "wages" paid to trust beneficiaries/related parties that don't reflect real services, since this can otherwise be used to disguise what is really a distribution.
- **Relevant flag for this specific venture:** both Anthony (explicitly "non-operational") and Imara ("has no operational involvement," per this venture's own standing facts — the Venue Manager covers all on-site duties) are currently documented as NOT performing hands-on work. Under the current documented model, this mechanism would need one of them to genuinely take on some operational role first — it is not simply available "because they own the business."

**General tax treatment:** ordinary wage income, taxed at the individual's marginal rate, same as any employee, with super paid on top (not instead of).

---

## Mechanism 3 — Director/Trustee Fees

**What it is:** the trustee company (YETI Tipi Holdings PTY LTD) can pay a fee to its director(s) for carrying out trustee/directorship duties — distinct from a distribution (which comes from trust income to a beneficiary) and distinct from a wage (which is for operational work).

**General mechanics:**
- Paid by the corporate trustee to whoever holds the director role, for genuinely performing director-level duties (governance, decision-making, signing authority, compliance oversight) — not for day-to-day operational work (that's Mechanism 2's territory).
- Like wages, this requires PAYG withholding and is assessable income to the recipient — it is not automatically concessionally taxed just because it's called a "director fee" rather than a "wage."
- Needs to be commercially reasonable for the actual governance work involved, for the same ATO-scrutiny reasons as Mechanism 2.

**Where this could apply:** Anthony's role as Trustee (of YETI Holding Trust) and any directorship of YETI Tipi Holdings PTY LTD (the corporate trustee) is the kind of role this mechanism is designed for — but whether this is genuinely available and at what rate needs direct accountant confirmation against the trust deed and company constitution, not assumed here.

---

## Mechanism 4 — Combination Approach

In practice, businesses running through a trust structure often use more than one of the above simultaneously — e.g. a modest trustee fee for governance work, plus the bulk of profit distributed as beneficiary income at year-end, splitting across whichever beneficiaries make the most tax-effective sense given each person's other income for that year. This is not a separate mechanism, just a reminder that the above are not mutually exclusive, and the actual optimal mix depends on both individuals' full financial pictures for the year in question — which is exactly the kind of modelling a real accountant needs to do with real numbers, not something to be decided in this document.
